“Oh, guys, don’t slow down!” chirped the Princess, dragging the whole team behind her. “You can’t think only about business all the time! You need to relax sometimes too. Why are you all so grumpy? Come on, let’s hang out—look at this gorgeous fair!”
“This is an amusement park,” noted the Half-Elf.
“Well, you see! When was the last time you were on a carousel? Let’s go have some fun.”
“This is a goblin amusement park,” clarified the Dwarf. “And I’ll never get on those carousels in my life!”
“Why?” the Halfling wondered. “You’re an experienced mechanic, aren’t you? I thought mechanical devices shouldn’t scare you, since you know how they’re built and how reliable everything is.”
“It’s exactly that knowledge that keeps me from trusting carousels,” replied the Dwarf. “Especially goblin ones.”
“It seems like in this world, goblins aren’t as malicious as they are everywhere else,” the Half-Elf said with some doubt.
“That may very well be,” the Dwarf didn’t argue. “But a goblin is still a goblin, whether wild or cultured, malicious or peaceful. Their character is always nasty, and their sense of humor… it’s… specific.”
A bat-eared goblin in an amusement park guide uniform approached the heroes with a welcoming, sharp-toothed smile.
“Can I help you with something, respected ones?”
“Yes!” the Princess rejoiced. “You can! Please tell us what you have here.”
“And how much it costs,” added the Halfling.
“Oh, we have dozens of entertainments for every taste!” the goblin’s smile grew even wider. “Sack races, log running, pie-eating competitions…”
“Pies with what?” the Barbarian perked up.
“Today with mushrooms. Champignons, if that’s important to you. Well, at least we call these mushrooms champignons. A very fun competition, I recommend it.”
“What else?”
“More? You can also try your luck climbing poles, test your strength on the strength meter…”
“Is that where you have to smash a button with a hammer?” the Dwarf’s eyes lit up excitedly.
“In principle, you could use a hammer,” nodded the guide. “But actually, I meant arm wrestling with a troll.”
“Whoa!” the Barbarian raised his eyebrows. “And how often does some goblin manage to beat the troll?”
“Oh, come on,” chuckled the guide. “None of ours bother with such nonsense. For goblins, we have a separate entrance—over there, to the stands, where you can watch all sorts of humans or elves tumbling off logs, getting tangled in sacks, and choking on pies. A delightful spectacle!”
“Well, no, I won’t agree to be a laughingstock for goblins!” the Dwarf bristled. “Go find a fool!”
“Oh, why bother searching!” the goblin waved it off serenely. “There’s always plenty of those. Alright, if you don’t want the publicity—no problem, we have closed pavilions too. Over there in that black tent, for example, the Room of Fear.”
The faces of all the heroes turned toward the black tent.
“Ah, I know,” declared the Halfling. “That’s a place where skeletons fall out of the walls at you, right?”
“And what’s scary about skeletons?” the goblin asked in surprise. “Everyone has a skeleton—even inside you, there’s one just like that.”
“But there, the skeletons move!”
“Yours moves too.”
The Halfling pondered.
“In this pavilion,” continued the guide, “you’ll encounter your hidden fears, individual phobias, and personal demons. In short, each of you will be met by exactly what you fear most in the world.”
“Sounds suspicious,” frowned the Half-Elf. “Somehow inhumane.”
“Well, we are goblins, after all.”
“Oh, to hell with it,” the Halfling shook his head. “I’m not going in there!”
“And I would go,” the Barbarian said dreamily. “Beating up your Biggest Fear—what a thrill!”
“Well, my Biggest Fear won’t fit in that tent,” the Dwarf scratched his beard thoughtfully, glancing at the Room of Fear.
“And I don’t fear anything at all!” the Half-Elf declared proudly. “So there’s definitely nothing for me to do there—don’t even try to persuade me.”
“You know,” the Princess swallowed nervously, alternately blushing and paling, “I probably pass too. If there really is what I fear most in the world… no, I don’t want to, thank you! And what’s in that pavilion over there, the pink one?”
“That’s the Room of Laughter,” the guide informed her readily. “Would you like to try it?”
“Ah, I know!” the Halfling showed his knowledge again. “There are crooked mirrors placed all over the room, right?”
“A crooked mirror isn’t funny,” shrugged the guide. “That’s sad. No, in the Room of Laughter, ten goblins will simply grab you, tie you to a chair, and tickle your heels. That’s more reliable.”
The Princess stared at the bright pink pavilion with wide-open eyes, shifted her gaze to the black tent, back to the pink, and again to the black.
“I don’t understand—is this the same Room?!”

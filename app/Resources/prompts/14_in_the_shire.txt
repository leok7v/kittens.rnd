• We’ll soon reach the borders of the Shire! - The Halfling sniffed the air and blissfully closed his eyes. - Can you smell it? Home, sweet home, the homeland of my ancestors…
 • The Shire is the place where halflings live, right? - asked the Princess.
 • That’s right, - nodded the Halfling. - But please call them hobbits. “Halfling” is considered an offensive word in the Shire.
 • Why? - the Barbarian was surprised. - You really are halflings. I mean, look at yourself and at me… or at the Princess over there.
 • First of all, I’m not a shorty, you two are giants. And secondly… - he hesitated, - I’m not the most typical representative of hobbits. According to the latest data, the average height in the Shire today is one meter sixty-eight centimeters. Acceleration.
 • Not that much, - remarked the Barbarian.
 • Well, no way, not much! - the Dwarf exclaimed indignantly. - Half a head taller than me! And you call that an “average hobbit”?!
 • I’ll tell you more, buddy, - the Halfling grinned broadly. - Some hobbits even have beards longer than yours!
 • Don’t lie, hobbits don’t grow beards! - the Half-elf frowned. - I know that for sure! You only have hair on your heads and feet.
The Halfling whistled and crossed his arms over his chest.
 • What outdated information you have! Let it be known to you that every fourth hobbit has a beard, every seventh has fangs, and sixty percent have smooth legs. Moreover, there are hobbits completely covered in fur, there are pale ones, swarthy ones, and completely black ones, with pointed and round ears; some live in burrows, others on the ground, and still others in trees. In every third family, they speak or spoke Rohanese, many have a Gondorian accent, and some continue to use the Orcish dialect. So don’t tell me that hobbits don’t have something. We have everything!
He straightened his jacket and added sternly:
 • And I would ask you to refrain from any offensive insinuations while we’re guests in the Shire. Our people are very proud of their ethnic identity. And their adherence to age-old traditions, yes. Very proud!
